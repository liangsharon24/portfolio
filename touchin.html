TouchIn

Connect Patients with Alzheimer's disease to other people.

Project Overview

This is an group project for student design competition of Computer-Human Interaction. The topic of the competition is "Assistive Technology." Our group focused on the needs of patients with Alzheimer's disease and their families, and designed an interface that facilitates the social communications of patients with Alzheimer's disease.

Design Process

Need Finding

Background

3.6 millions elderly people in the U.S. are home-bound. Alzheimer's disease and dementia are the primary reason causing home-bound. Abundant research has demonstrated that social interaction could help prevent dementia and Alzheimer’s disease. However, patients with Alzheimer's disease and their families often report social isolations. Our goal in this project is to develop an assistive technology to help patients with Alzheimer’s disease build social connections with people outside their social circle.

Understand the Pain Point of Patients and Their Families

We interviewed 2 seniors with Alzheimer's disease and their families. From the hours interviews, We found that the things they concerned the most are:

as his brain deteriorate to the point when he doesn’t know me, he doesn’t take care of his own, he will go down there.
Patients’ declination in cognitive ability and independence.
He enjoys being out where other people are. Even though he's not involved in what's going on.
Patients’ active or passive withdrawal from social interaction.
I couldn’t pursue my career, I couldn’t pursue friends and things
Caregiver’s stress due to heavy loading of caring for the patients without much break.

Brainstorming the solution

After collecting data from the interviews, online forums and papers, we hold several brainstorming sessions to discuss the possible designs. The design solution was approached from different perspectives such as:

  • A financial app to help them manage the expense of life.
  • Devices that help them exercise on a daily basis.
  • Games that can maintain their attentions by interacting with them and give them suggestion when they are confused.

By presenting the proposals and collecting feedbacks, we decide to focus on:

  • Increasing the social interaction for people live alone in the house.
  • A product that can maintain their attention for a period of time.

Prototype and Testing

The initial concept of the product was a system containing a video camera and an application on the ipad that would give patients hints when they were stuck at what to do next.

Iteration1: Can User Interact with a unfamiliar interface?

How would user interact with people in the screen?

The patients would try to use an App called Colorfy that they can color the picture on the screen. At the same time, a moderator would talk with them through the Skype video call.

The goal of this testing is to understand:

  • Whether Alzheimer’s patients can interact with people through a Skype video call.
  • How Alzheimer’s patients would react to a coloring application on an electronic device (iPad).
What kind of hints is effective for seniors with memory difficulties?

Five types of hints on a coloring task that indicated where to start was presented to the participants, and how they react with these hints were recorded.

The goal is to understand:

  • Whether Alzheimer’s patients need other people help them to start a task, and help them decide which color they should use.
  • If Alzheimer’s patients need an initiation for their task, which kind of hints works best for them?
Reflection
  1. Elder people need someone else to accompanied at first to help them be familiar with the technology.
  2. They would like to try the coloring game on ipad. However, if there were too many color choices or there were too many steps to do for changing a color, they felt confusing.
  3. Their need for assistance was especially strong when they are using an unfamiliar device.
  4. None of the hints we designed works for the patients. They knew it was a hint but would neglect the hint and use their own way to do the task.

Iteration 2: How to improve participants’ understanding about how the interface?

Approach: The moderator also holds an ipad with identical screen and demonstrates the steps to the participant through video call.

Finding:

  1. The participant often got stuck at selecting a color because the interface did not provide clear feedback about which color was chosen.
  2. Participants showed a willingness to interact with people in the screen.
  3. It required an attention shift of the participants between the iPad screen and the laptop screen, which might be overloading to the participant.

Iteration 3: How to improve the quality of interaction between the moderator and the participant

Approach: The video image was overlapping with the canvas. It not only decreases the devices required for the system but also provides an interaction that is closer to face-to-face interaction.

We used the app, DrawTime, which provides similar function, to test our concept.

Findings:

  1. The participants still struggled with changing color by touching the color palette on the screen.
  2. The participant felt confused about the instruction of moderators because the location of the camera resulted in an inconsistency of the hand position between where the moderator pointed to and where it was presented on the screen of the participant’s ipad.

Final design

The final design is two sets of devices customized to the need of moderators and users.

For the volunteers:

  • An application that can be installed on any present devices including laptop, tablet and smart phone.
  • A fingerstall for the volunteer. By drawing in the sky in front of the screen, the camera can capture the correct hand position.

For the patients:

  • A tablet with a camera.
  • Styluses with different colors. Thus, the participant could just pick up the stylus with the color she or he want. Physical pen would decrease the learning curve for the patients when using our product.
